Mark began his career in Emergency Management in 2000, where he worked for Ada City-County Emergency Management, Boise, Idaho, as Exercise and Training Coordinator. Robertson was also a Regional Coordinator for the Idaho Department of Homeland Security Exercise and Evaluation Program and facilitator and evaluator for exercises held at the National Interagency Fire Center.

Mark was the State of Nebraska National Incident Management System (NIMS) Coordinator and Exercise Training Officer for the Nebraska Emergency Management Agency and worked with Nebraska Office of the Chief Information Officer as Disaster and Continuity of Operations Coordinator.

Currently, Mark is the Emergency Management Coordinator for the University of Nebraska - Lincoln. This season will be his eighteenth as the Planning Section Chief of the Incident Management Team for Nebraska football games, which sees regular attendance at 90,000 people, with the longest sellout streak in the nation (since 1962).

Mark admits to being an Incident Command System (ICS) nerd and beginning his career as a member of the media but asks to not hold that against him. Presently, Mark instructs three courses for NCS4 and has been a key member of the training team since beginning approximately two years ago.

  • Title:

    Emergency Management Director

  • Current Organization:

    University Police Department, University of Nebraska - Lincoln

  • What courses do you teach?

    Public Information and Emergency Notification, Sport Event Risk Management, and Sport and Special Event Evacuation Training and Exercise.

  • What motivated you to become and NCS4 Trainer?

    To share best practices for safety and security with those that have the responsibility for those roles in the events they manage and support.

  • What has been the greatest professional benefit to instructing with NCS4?

    The saying goes “if you want to really learn something – teach it”. This has been tremendously true for me, I learn from presenting the materials, but learn more from the students that attend the courses and share their experiences and best practices.

  • What is your most memorable location or experience from a delivery?

    The first course I instructed was in the Golden Nugget Casino in Atlantic City (because, why not?) I stayed at there at the Casino and the first morning, in the elevator on the way to class, a gambler couple wished me good luck… I remember thinking, “I am going to need it!”

  • What is one takeaway from any NCS4 course you wish you knew as a young professional?

    From all of the courses: “it is not who is in charge, but who is in charge of what”. Big events require a great deal of coordination and planning from all stakeholders. The better stakeholders work together to define roles and responsibilities, in the planning phase, the better prepared everyone is for the event and potential incidents. The courses simulate the coordination that occurs when stakeholders work together.

  • Why do you feel these courses are important to the security industry and the nation?

    The courses are an ideal way for stakeholders to determine best practices for their individual jurisdictions and the events held in their cities. The material is applicable to all events whether it is a small county fair or a nationally televised sporting event. The exercises in the courses lay the foundation for improvement of event management and response.


“The experience of working with NCS4 has been remarkably valuable. I learn from the co-instructors and the students. I am able to bring back creative solutions to deal with areas that need improvement. I encourage anyone involved with sports management or response to consider attending. I am certain you will find the experience to be worthwhile.” – Mark Roberston
